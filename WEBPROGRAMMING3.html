INTRODUCTION TO INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY:
Information technology (IT) is the use of any computers, storage, networking and other physical devices, infrastructure and processes to create, process, store, secure and exchange all forms of electronic data.
We started by learning about Computational thinking and what the processes are.



Computational thinking is a problem-solving and cognitive skill set that involves approaching complex
problems in a way that a computer, or a human with a computational mindset, might tackle them. It's a fundamental
skill in computer science and is applicable to a wide range of disciplines beyond just programming.

Key components of computational thinking include:

Decomposition: Breaking down a complex problem or system into smaller, more manageable parts or components.

Pattern Recognition: Identifying similarities and patterns within problems to facilitate the development of general solutions.

Abstraction: Removing unnecessary details to focus on the key elements of a problem. This involves creating models or representations that capture the essential aspects.

Algorithmic Thinking: Developing step-by-step instructions or procedures for solving a problem. Algorithms are precise sets of instructions for solving specific problems.

Debugging and Troubleshooting: The ability to find and fix errors or problems in a solution. This is a crucial aspect of computational thinking, as it involves identifying and correcting mistakes in algorithms and code.

Computational thinking is not limited to computer scientists; it's a valuable skill for anyone involved in problem-solving across various domains. It encourages a systematic and logical approach to problem-solving, helping individuals think more clearly and rigorously about problems, whether they are related to technology, science, business, or everyday life.
